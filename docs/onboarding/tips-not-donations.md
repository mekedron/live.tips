# You are collecting tips, not donations

When you set up your Stripe account, Stripe asks what your business does. It is
worth spending two minutes on that answer, because Stripe treats **tips** and
**donations** as two different businesses — and only one of them describes you.

Stripe's own rule of thumb:

- A **tip** is given for a good or service that has **already been provided**.
- A **donation** must be tied to a **specific charitable purpose** you are
  committing to accomplish.

You played the set. The fan watched it, enjoyed it, and paid you for it. That is
a tip — a gratuity for a live performance, the same thing as the coins that used
to land in the case, and the same thing as the tip line on a restaurant bill. It
is not a donation, you are not a charity, and you are not fundraising for a cause.

## Why this matters

Stripe keeps a
[list of restricted businesses](https://stripe.com/legal/restricted-businesses).
"Organisations fundraising for a charitable purpose" sits on it, and outside
Australia, Canada, the UK and the US it is listed as **prohibited** — which
covers most of Europe, where most buskers are. So an account that *looks* like
charitable fundraising can get extra review, or be turned down, even though the
thing you actually do — busking for tips — is ordinary and allowed.

None of that is a problem for a performer who describes themselves as a
performer. It only becomes a problem if the paperwork says "donations" when the
reality is "I play music and people tip me."

## What to do

- **Describe the real business.** "Live music performance", "street performer",
  "musician — tips and gratuities from audiences at live performances". Say that
  you perform, and that the payments are tips for those performances.
- **Pick a category that matches.** Something in the live entertainment /
  performing arts direction, not "charity", "non-profit", or "fundraising".
- **Do not call it a donation** on your Stripe account, your website, or your
  sign, unless you genuinely are raising money for a charitable cause — in which
  case the rules above apply to you and you should read them properly.
- If you *are* also raising money for a cause, keep it separate from your tip
  jar and talk to Stripe about it first.

live.tips is built around this: the app names the Stripe Product it creates after
your act, describes it as tips for a live performance, and does not use Stripe's
"donate" checkout mode. The account itself, though, is yours — Stripe asks *you*
what you do, and only you can answer.

## The honest caveat

This is not legal advice, and it is not tax advice. Whether your account is
approved is **Stripe's decision**, not ours — live.tips has no relationship with
Stripe, no ability to influence a review, and no way to appeal one on your behalf.
Rules also differ by country and change over time. If real money or your
livelihood depends on the answer, read Stripe's current
[restricted businesses list](https://stripe.com/legal/restricted-businesses) and
[requirements for accepting tips or donations](https://support.stripe.com/questions/requirements-for-accepting-tips-or-donations)
yourself, and ask a professional in your own country if you are unsure.

What we can tell you is this: a tip for a gig you actually played is a tip. Say
so, plainly, and the rest of the setup is boring.
